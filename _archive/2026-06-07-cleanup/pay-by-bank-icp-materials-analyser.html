Pay by Bank · Ideal Customer Profile

Who Pay by Bank is for

High-risk merchants — first iGaming, casino and betting — in the UK and EU/EEA, with 100k+ transactions a month, where card friction, settlement delay, chargebacks or payment cost hit margin and operations. Market experiments such as nightlife are documented separately, so the proven core stays sharp.

Owner: Commercial · Confidential

The customer in one line

A UK or EU/EEA high-risk merchant — primarily iGaming, casino or betting — with 100k+ transactions a month, where card friction, settlement delay, chargeback exposure or payment cost materially affects margin and operations.

Definition

The ideal Pay by Bank customer is a UK or European merchant or payment-driven platform where incoming customer payments directly affect margin, settlement speed, cash flow, customer experience, fraud exposure, reconciliation or daily operations.

Pay by Bank covers the customer → merchant direction only — checkout, deposits, top-ups, instant customer funding. If the customer mainly needs outgoing payments (player payouts, or refunds as a standalone flow), it is not an Aryze fit today.

This customer is not looking for another checkout option. They have a real payment problem that costs them money, time or customers.

Where it runs

  • Scope: UK + EU/EEA merchants. Mastercard Open Banking rails cover 19 European markets and more than 2,900 banks.
  • Staged rollout: UK is live via the Volt.io partnership. The EU/EEA rollout begins late Q2 2026, pending the Volt-extension contract. Until then, EU prospects are pipeline conversations — not commit-to-go-live timelines.
  • Bank depth varies by country — confirmed per customer, never guessed.

Best-fit company profile

  • Operates in the UK or EU/EEA
  • Significant incoming payment volume — sweet spot 100k+ transactions a month, average ticket EUR 35+
  • Depends heavily on digital payments
  • Feels cost, friction, delay or operational problems in card-based flows
  • Payment performance sits on the leadership agenda, not in the back office
  • Has a clear internal owner (payments, finance, product, risk or operations)
  • Has the technical capacity to integrate a new payment rail

Strongest verticals, in order

All within the high-risk frame:

  1. iGaming, casinos, betting — the core segment and primary focus. High deposit frequency, high chargeback exposure, expensive card fees, and pressure from Visa VAMP (the 1.5% threshold from April 2026).
  2. Trading platforms — instant-funding flows, high average ticket, payment performance directly business-critical.
  3. Other high-risk merchants — categories where card rails are expensive, fragile or restricted, or where acquiring is hard to get.
  4. Secondary (longer tail): wallets, remittance, e-commerce with high average ticket or poor card economics — only where the high-risk pain is clear.

Outside the ICP — separate market-entry cases. Nightlife and events (bars, clubs, festivals) share the same high-risk pain and were analysed as a new market in May–June 2026, with a board decision on a summer pilot in Denmark and the UK. The case is kept separate so it stays clear what is proven core and what is experiment: Nightlife — Pay by Bank.

What the customer wants

"Get paid faster, with less friction and more control."

This customer does not want a future-of-money story. They want a better payment operating model: lower cost, faster settlement, less dispute burden, cleaner reconciliation, and better control over incoming funds.

Buying triggers

A prospect becomes a strong fit when at least one of these is active:

  • Card costs are too high and eat margin
  • Settlement is too slow for cash flow
  • Chargebacks and disputes create financial and operational burden
  • Refund handling is painful
  • Reconciliation creates too much manual work
  • Payment status visibility is weak
  • The current payment flow is too indirect to control
  • The business needs instant or near-instant customer funding (deposits, top-ups)
  • Card flows fit the customer journey poorly
  • Payment performance has been discussed at leadership level — the strongest signal of all

The five pains we solve

  1. High payment costs — card fees scale with the amount. Scheme fees have risen 75%+ since 2021 for large merchants.
  2. Slow settlement — money is not available in time to support cash flow, liquidity or operations.
  3. Operational friction — refunds, reporting, reconciliation and payment-status tracking create work for finance and ops.
  4. Chargeback and dispute burden — iGaming runs 2–4% chargeback rates against 0.5–1% in normal e-commerce. Cost per dispute: EUR 20–100, with a loaded cost around 2.5× the transaction value.
  5. Lack of control — insufficient visibility across the payment flow from initiation to settlement.

Strong-fit signals

  • Payment performance is discussed at leadership level
  • Card fees are visible in the payment P&L
  • Chargebacks, fraud or disputes are operationally relevant
  • Settlement speed has real cash-flow impact
  • There is an internal payment owner
  • Payment operations are already painful or strategically important
  • The business is actively looking for alternatives to card rails
  • Deposits, top-ups or customer-funding flows are commercially important
  • There is technical capacity to integrate a new rail

Walk-away signals

  • The business operates outside the UK + EU/EEA
  • Payment volume is low or low-priority
  • Card costs are not a meaningful issue
  • Settlement speed does not matter to them
  • They just want "more payment methods"
  • No internal payment owner exists
  • The business cannot support an integration
  • No clear pain around cost, settlement, reconciliation, chargebacks or control
  • The primary need is outgoing payments (payouts, withdrawals, standalone refunds) — we cover the incoming direction only. If the use case can be split, we take the deposit side now and flag payouts as open.
  • They want AIS features (account aggregation, balance checks, account data) — we are PIS-only, no AIS at all
  • They want standalone recurring payments — only possible via Mastercard Payment Services DK/NO until PSD3 (estimated 2027–28)

Qualification questions for the first call

  1. How many transactions a month today? Average ticket?
  2. What total card fee % do you pay (acquiring + scheme + interchange)? How much has it risen since 2021?
  3. Where is your chargeback rate today? Do you feel pressure from the VAMP 1.5% threshold from April 2026?
  4. Which markets are critical — UK only, or specific EU countries?
  5. Which PSP / payment stack do you run today? Do you already have an open-banking provider (Trustly, TrueLayer, Volt, Token, Banked)?
  6. What ranks highest among your pains: cost, chargebacks, conversion, settlement speed, or operations/reconciliation?
  7. Who joins the decision — Payments, Finance, Risk, Tech, Compliance?
  8. Do you have the engineering capacity and timeline to integrate a new rail?

Write the answers in the CRM note. Do not pitch anything concrete before you have answers to at least #1, #2 and #5.

How we position it

"A direct UK/EU account-to-account payment rail that helps merchants get paid faster, with lower friction and stronger operational control than card-based flows."

Stay close to payment performance. No abstract innovation story — this customer needs a better way to get paid, not a new financial paradigm.

Market context — June 2026

  • Visa VAMP drops to 1.5% in April 2026 (acquirers held at 0.5–0.7%). Real pressure on iGaming chargeback rates that typically run 2–4%.
  • Scheme fees up 75%+ since 2021 for large merchants — margin pressure on card-based flows.
  • UK A2A pricing benchmark: down to GBP 0.50 fixed per transaction against roughly 4% average on cards.
  • A2A is only about 5% of UK e-commerce — an early market with room for early movers.
  • The Open Banking PISP licence framework is mature; Mastercard runs AISP + PISP across Europe and the UK with 99.99%+ uptime.

The rule: never "we are better than Trustly" without concrete features. Acknowledge they exist, then make room for Aryze on the specific job the customer has that is not solved today — the Mastercard rail approach, coverage in specific markets, pricing structure, theming, or integration time. Never promise features we do not have because a competitor has them.

Capability limits — apply to all sales material

Aryze delivers PIS only (Payment Initiation Services), covering the customer → merchant direction. The following is not part of the offer today — be explicit if it comes up:

  • AIS features: account owner verification, balance checks, account aggregation, combined AIS+PIS flows, KYC via open banking. We have PIS only.
  • Payouts (merchant → customer): withdrawals, payouts and standalone refunds are not productised at Aryze. What we offer instead: we take the deposit / top-up / customer-funding side now, and flag payouts as open.
  • Standalone recurring payments: not possible under PSD2. Only via Mastercard Payment Services DK/NO. Standalone expected with PSD3 (estimated 2027–28).
  • Refunds: shown as a feature on the Mastercard platform — always check with product before promising refund flows in Aryze's setup.

If a customer refers to a Mastercard feature we do not have live, say so directly and log it as an open question in the CRM note. Never say "we can also do that" unless you are certain.
