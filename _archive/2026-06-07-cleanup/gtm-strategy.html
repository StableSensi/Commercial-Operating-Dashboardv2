Go-to-market: nightlife, summer 2026

The commercial plan for June, July and August — positioning, the two tracks, geography, targets, pricing posture, the three-month plan, success metrics and dependencies.

Owner: Commercial · 30 May 2026 · Confidential

↩ Package overview Source .md

Date: 30 May 2026

Markets: Lead generation across Denmark, the United Kingdom and the EU. Active execution starting in Denmark.

1. The play in one page

Aryze is expanding from iGaming into nightlife, bars, clubs and events as a parallel vertical, alongside iGaming rather than instead of it. This document is the commercial plan for the first three months.

The strategy rests on a small number of decisions, each settled through deliberate review rather than assumed.

We lead on chargeback elimination and reliability, not on price. The pitch opens on the structural elimination of card chargebacks, the regulatory pain of merchant category code (MCC) 5813, and the strength of running on Mastercard Open Banking rails. Price is a supporting argument. At realistic Danish ticket sizes (an average purchase around DKK 150) the fixed fee already beats cards, so price is a win we state plainly and then move past, not a battle we fight.

We prove the model before we chase scale. The near-term goal is not revenue. It is live transactions, real data and a small number of named reference venues. That proof is the asset. It is also the key that unlocks the larger players, who will not engage with an unproven rail.

We start direct, owner to owner, and we use our network. The primary motion is one-to-one outreach to bar and club owners, accelerated by warm introductions through the team's industry network. Nightlife is a relationship business, and a warm introduction is worth more than any volume of cold outreach.

We sell the rail, not a build. We sell the account-to-account (A2A) rail and let the venue decide how to put it in front of its guests.

Denmark is both home market and proving ground. It is where we are strongest, where the network gives us warm access, and where one relationship can deliver scale and distribution at once. Execution starts here and rolls outward as the model becomes repeatable.

2. Positioning and the core pitch

The pitch runs in a fixed order: chargebacks first, reliability second, price as support.

Chargeback elimination. Every card payment a venue takes can be disputed after the night. In Denmark the consumer has up to 120 days, and up to 13 months in cases of alleged misuse, and the bank refunds the customer by the end of the next business day, leaving the venue to prove the transaction. Persistent chargebacks push a venue onto Visa and Mastercard monitoring programmes (the Visa Acquirer Monitoring Program threshold tightened to 1.5% in April 2026) and ultimately risk the MATCH list, a five-year industry blacklist. A2A payments are push-initiated under strong customer authentication and carry no card scheme chargeback mechanism. This is a structural difference with no counter-argument.

Mastercard-grade reliability. The other A2A providers in this space are early-stage and small, and several do not own their rails. Aryze runs Pay by Bank on Mastercard Open Banking, connected to around 2,900 European banks. For any serious counterparty the relevant question is who will still be operating, and standing behind the service, in two years. We say the Mastercard backing out loud, because the venue sees the Aryze name, not the rails behind it.

Price. At realistic ticket sizes the fixed fee beats cards, and the foreign-card cost in tourist-heavy Copenhagen and London venues is highest in summer. Price is a reason to feel comfortable, not the reason to switch.

The adjacent-market indication. In iGaming, an adjacent high-risk vertical, provider selection has not been driven by headline price alone. The market leader is not the cheapest option, which indicates that reliability, trust and embedded distribution weigh heavily in this kind of segment. This is an indication from a neighbouring market, not a proven rule for nightlife, but it supports a positioning built on risk elimination and reliability rather than lowest price.

The boundary. Aryze is a chargeback-free, reliable payment option alongside a venue's existing card setup, not a wholesale replacement. The venue keeps taking cards, especially from non-EEA tourists who cannot use A2A. The payment experience for the mainstream guest is no harder than the phone-based payments Danish consumers already use every day, so there is no adoption friction to overcome in the payment itself. The one narrow exception is the affluent card-and-points customer at the top end, who pays by card for rewards and is not our target.

3. The two tracks

Track B, primary: direct to venues

One-to-one outreach to bar and club owners and operators. Owner-led, relationship-led, accelerated by the team's warm industry network, which includes recognised artist and operator relationships in the segment, notably a direct relationship with the rapper Jokeren (Jesper Dahl), who is well connected across Danish nightlife. A warm introduction delivers access and credibility at the same time.

The near-term objective of Track B is the proof, not the revenue: live transactions, real numbers on chargebacks avoided, the actual ticket sizes captured, conversion at the point of sale, and a small set of named reference venues. Per-venue revenue is steady and scales across venues. The lighthouse case is the asset that unlocks everything after it.

Track A, opportunistic and gated: the larger players

Wallet and loyalty providers, point-of-sale platforms, and venue-group consolidators. The governing rule is simple and realistic: no transactions, no large players. The big players will not bet on an unproven rail. Track A therefore waits on the proof from Track B, with one deliberate exception below.

We open Track A conversations where we are already present, for example at relevant industry conferences, but we expect nothing from them until the proof exists. Once it does, the conversation with a consolidator turns from "would you try something new" into "here is what it did in real venues, now switch your rail."

The Denmark exception

In Denmark, home market and warm network mean we open the largest-player conversation now, in parallel with Track B, rather than waiting. The clearest example is Rekom, which owns both a large venue estate and the NightPay loyalty and top-up wallet that currently runs on card rails. One relationship there could deliver a concrete rail swap (replacing the card-based top-up with Aryze's A2A rail, cheaper and chargeback-free, with Aryze as the rail under their existing loyalty layer) and distribution across their venues. The network opens that door early. The proof from Track B is still what closes it. Door first, evidence second, both in motion now.

4. Geography: broad pipeline, focused execution

Lead generation is broad. Over the three months we build a pipeline across Denmark, the United Kingdom and the EU. Building a target list, identifying venues and making light first contact can run broadly without heavy resourcing.

Active pursuit and pilots are focused and roll outward. Execution starts in Denmark, because it is home market and because the warm-introduction engine converts fastest there. As the model becomes proven and repeatable, the active motion expands into the United Kingdom and then selected EU markets. The warm-introduction network is specific to Denmark, so the UK and EU are colder and naturally slower, which is the practical reason the heavy effort begins at home and spreads outward.

Large players follow the same logic with the Denmark exception. Open Rekom and NightPay now in Denmark via the network. In the UK and the EU, large players wait until transactions are proven.

6. Pricing posture

Standard pricing is DKK 3 (approximately EUR 0.40) in the Nordics and EUR 0.50 in the wider EU. A mainstream tier of DKK 2 to 2.5 is available where a sharper price point is useful, and it requires sign-off from product and commercial before it is quoted.

The posture is consistent with the positioning. We lead on chargebacks and reliability. Price is a supporting point that already wins against cards at realistic ticket sizes. We do not open on price and we do not discount to win, because the value we sell is not the cheapest transaction, it is the elimination of chargeback risk on a reliable rail.

7. The three months

A small commercial team of two to three people can run a focused Denmark execution track and a broad, light lead-generation track at the same time.

June: foundation, first conversations, doors opened

  • Confirm the mainstream pricing tier with product and commercial.
  • Confirm the Pay by Bank rail demo (a live A2A payment) is ready to show.
  • Finalise the chargeback-led pitch and a one-page leave-behind, citing the market analysis for evidence.
  • Map the first five to ten warm network introductions to Danish venue owners, and begin those conversations.
  • Open the Rekom and NightPay conversation through the network, in parallel.
  • Begin broad lead generation across the UK and the EU (list and light first contact), without committing execution effort there yet.
  • Target by month-end: one signed lighthouse pilot commitment in Denmark.

July: pilots live, real data, peak season

  • Launch one to two Danish lighthouse pilots. The job of the pilot is data: transaction count, the ticket sizes actually captured, the measured chargeback difference, and the card cost displaced.
  • July and August are peak operating season. That is good for transaction data and for the foreign-card cost that strengthens the pitch, and it is hard for operator attention, because owners are busy. Run the pilots now, expect buying decisions to land in late August and September.
  • Continue Danish outreach. Keep feeding the Rekom and NightPay conversation with early pilot signals as they appear.
  • Continue UK and EU lead generation.

August: evidence, conversion, set up the autumn

  • Turn the pilot data into a lighthouse case: real numbers on chargebacks avoided, cost displaced, and ticket sizes captured.
  • Convert the warmest Danish pipeline using that evidence.
  • With proof in hand, begin opening the gated UK and EU large-player and platform conversations, and open the 2027 festival-platform conversations (the 2026 festival season is already contracted, so festivals are a 2027 motion, not a summer one).
  • Review against the metrics below and decide where to concentrate in the autumn.

8. Success metrics

Tracked weekly, reviewed at each month-end.

  • Warm introductions activated and venue conversations opened in Denmark.
  • Lighthouse pilots live (target: one to two in Denmark by end of July).
  • Pilot evidence captured: transaction volume, average ticket captured, measured chargeback difference, card cost displaced.
  • Rekom and NightPay conversation opened and advancing.
  • Breadth of the UK and EU lead pipeline built for the autumn.

The primary summer deliverable is a credible lighthouse case with real numbers, plus a pipeline. Venue-level revenue is steady and scales across venues. The case is what unlocks the larger players and the scale motion.

9. Risks and boundaries

  • The proof gates almost everything. Until Track B produces live transactions and a case, the larger players stay out of reach (except the Denmark network exception). The plan is sequenced around this on purpose.
  • Festivals are a 2027 motion. The largest single opportunity in the market analysis cannot be captured this summer because the 2026 season is contracted. We start that work in August, aimed at 2027.
  • The affluent top end is not the target. The card-and-points customer at VIP level will not switch. We target the broad evening spend, not the VIP table.
  • The network is Denmark-specific. It is why Denmark moves fastest and why the UK and EU are slower and start with lead generation rather than pilots.
  • Pricing sign-off is pending. The mainstream tier needs approval before it is quoted.
  • We are building the category. Being first means there is no competitor playbook to copy. The pilots are how we write it.
