Pay by Bank in nightlife, bars, clubs, festivals and events

A market analysis across the Nordics, the wider EU and the UK — size and structure, the cost of the card category, chargeback and fraud, transaction values, the solution landscape, fit and geographic priority.

1. Executive summary

This report assesses the market for Pay by Bank across nightlife, bars, clubs, festivals and event venues in the Nordics, the wider EU and the UK. The opportunity is volume: a fixed fee per transaction, structurally cheaper than card acquiring and competitive against established account-to-account (A2A) specialists, earned across the whole customer base rather than a narrow top tier.

Four findings carry the analysis.

The segment is structurally high risk. Nightlife venues operate under merchant category code (MCC) 5813 (Drinking Places, Bars, Taverns, Cocktail Lounges, Nightclubs and Discotheques). Acquirers apply this classification consistently across Europe. It produces elevated processing fees, rolling reserves and chargeback exposure relative to standard hospitality. These costs are documented at the acquirer and regulatory level.

The aggregate market is substantial but uneven. The European pubs, bars and coffee shops sector reached EUR 100.6 billion in 2025 (IBISWorld). National venue populations run into the tens of thousands (approximately 50,000 establishments in Spain, 33,911 enterprises in Germany). The UK nightclub population has contracted sharply, down 36% since March 2020 to 823 venues in 2025, and average spend per visit varies by an order of magnitude across markets, from EUR 14 per attendance in mainstream Italian discotheques to EUR 250 and above per visit in Ibiza.

No A2A provider has built a dedicated position in the segment. Pay by Bank solutions are established in adjacent service industries (restaurants, hotels, ticketing platforms). The specific segment of nightlife, late-night bars, clubs and event venues is not currently served by a dedicated A2A provider with named customer cases. Festival cashless wristband platforms route their underlying top-up flow through card rails.

The commercial model is driven by volume. At Aryze's working pricing of approximately EUR 0.40 per transaction in the Nordics (DKK 3) and EUR 0.50 per transaction in the wider EU, the fixed fee is cheaper than card acquiring at almost any transaction value above approximately EUR 15 to EUR 25, and competitive against the cheapest A2A specialists from roughly EUR 70 up. Because the fee is the same on every transaction, the revenue is driven by transaction count across the whole customer base. The largest volume pools are festival and event cashless top-ups, mainstream high-frequency venues, mid-tier event ticketing, and business-to-business supplier payments.

The single largest opportunity identified is the festival and event cashless top-up rail, where the underlying flow is currently card based across the market and where one platform relationship aggregates volume across many events.

The market evidence supports moving forward into a focused commercial phase.

2. Scope and methodology

Strategic context. Aryze is examining whether nightlife, bars, clubs, festivals and events is a vertical where Pay by Bank has a clearer market opportunity. This is a parallel exploration alongside iGaming, not a move away from it.

This report covers mainstream nightlife: licensed nightclubs, late-night bars, cocktail venues, music venues, festivals, event venues and adjacent venue-based payment flows. Adult entertainment, pornographic businesses and strip-club venues are excluded from all sizing, classification and ranking in this report.

Operating coverage. Aryze provides Pay by Bank across the United Kingdom and the EU and EEA, reaching 19 European markets and more than 2,900 banks through Mastercard Open Banking. This report treats both the UK and the EU and EEA as operable markets.

Currency note: the Nordic working price of DKK 3 is shown throughout as approximately EUR 0.40 at prevailing rates. Country figures are quoted in their reporting currency with EUR equivalents where useful.

4. High-risk merchant classification and its cost

Nightlife venues operate under MCC 5813. Acquirer underwriting documentation classifies this code as medium to high risk and applies it consistently across Europe and globally. The structural reasons cited across multiple acquirer guides (PXP, Rapyd, eflow, Corepay) are consistent:

  1. Late-night transactions involving alcohol consumption.
  2. Tab-based billing, where the original authorisation differs from the final settled amount.
  3. High variance in transaction volume across the week and across seasons.
  4. First-party (friendly) fraud, where customers dispute transactions after the event.
  5. Event cancellation and refund disputes at venues with ticketed entry.

5. Chargeback and fraud profile

The chargeback environment is documented at two levels, the card scheme level and the regulatory level.

Scheme level

  • Visa Acquirer Monitoring Program (VAMP). From 1 April 2026, the merchant Excessive threshold dropped from 2.2% to 1.5% across the US, Canada and the EU, the monitoring floor moved to 1,500 combined fraud reports and disputes per month, and enforcement moved to a fee of USD 8 per disputed or fraudulent transaction with no warning tier (Visa, Merchant Risk Council). This tightening directly raises the cost of card disputes for high-volume venues.
  • Mastercard Excessive Chargeback Merchant (ECM). Thresholds are 100 or more chargebacks combined with a 1.5% ratio, persisting across two consecutive months.
  • MATCH list. Mastercard maintains a five-year industry record of terminated merchants. The database is not public, and excessive chargebacks are a dominant trigger for listing.

Regulatory level

  • Denmark. A customer can dispute a card payment for 120 days from becoming aware of the problem, and up to 13 months in cases of misuse. The bank refunds the customer no later than the end of the next business day after the dispute is filed (Finanstilsynet, Forbrugerombudsmanden, Konkurrence- og Forbrugerstyrelsen).
  • United Kingdom. Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act protects cardholders on purchases above GBP 100, with claims available up to six years.
  • European Union. PSD2 sets the framework for payment disputes and fraud liability.

Fraud composition

First-party (friendly) fraud accounts for an estimated 60 to 80% of all chargebacks across industries (Chargebacks911, Chargeflow 2025), and first-party fraud rose from 15% of reported fraud in 2023 to 36% in 2024. UK card-not-present fraud reached GBP 572.6 million in 2024 (UK Finance Annual Fraud Report 2025).

Documented sector failures

Two recent UK failures illustrate the chargeback exposure concentrated in event ticketing. Festicket entered administration in September 2022 owing GBP 22.5 million to creditors, with customers directed to chargeback their card issuers. Pollen collapsed in August 2022 owing GBP 78.6 million (IQ Magazine, Mixmag, BBC).

The A2A fraud position

Open Banking Limited reported in December 2025 that A2A payment fraud ran at 0.013% by volume against 0.045% for the wider payment industry in H1 2025, with the report drawing on data from March 2024 to September 2025 and from institutions representing over 60% of UK open banking payments. By value the figures were 0.020% against 0.027%. Both the volume and the value of open banking fraud fell year on year while industry-wide fraud rose. The report also notes that the open banking figure had risen to 0.023% by September 2025, and that authorised push payment fraud makes up 74% of open banking fraud cases. The structural advantage is real and recently reconfirmed, and it is narrowing at the margin rather than widening.

A2A payments are push-initiated under strong customer authentication and do not run through card scheme rails, so there is no card scheme chargeback mechanism. Consumer protection still applies through the regulatory channel (UK Financial Ombudsman, EU PSD2 rules on unauthorised transactions), and refund handling becomes a manual merchant process rather than a scheme-mandated reversal. The exposure that is eliminated is the scheme-based chargeback and the associated reserve. The exposure that remains is regulatory dispute handling and authorised push payment fraud.

A structural note on holding balances

A model that holds a customer's pre-loaded balance in a wallet brings that balance within electronic money regulation. A model that pushes funds directly from the customer's bank account to the venue's bank account does not. This distinction is relevant to the segment because several venue and festival payment products operate on a stored-balance wallet model. A direct push-to-bank A2A flow avoids the electronic-money classification by design.

What is not publicly available

A per-merchant chargeback rate specifically for MCC 5813 is not disclosed by acquirers or trade bodies. The high-risk classification is confirmed structurally at the scheme and acquirer documentation level. The magnitude of chargeback cost at an individual venue is a market characteristic that published data does not quantify, and figures circulated in payments blogs for nightlife chargeback rates are unsourced and are not relied on here.

Reading the gap

The absence of dedicated A2A coverage in this segment has two possible explanations. The first is that established providers prioritised verticals with a cleaner buyer experience (restaurants with table service, hotels with pre-booking, e-commerce with structured checkout), and deferred the operationally harder nightlife and event segment (high volume, late night, alcohol, fragmented merchants). The second is that the segment was deferred because the unit economics do not work.

The evidence favours the first reading. Hospitality A2A providers have not publicly tested the nightlife segment with dedicated product, and festival cashless platforms have not adopted A2A rails because their existing card-based contracts remain profitable. The existence of payment-first products built specifically for nightlife operators, such as the Danish NightPay (Rekom group), is a market signal that operators consider payment economics important enough to build proprietary systems. The second reading cannot be dismissed on published data alone, but the weight of evidence points to deferral by priority rather than by economics.

Conclusion of the landscape

No A2A provider has publicly built a dedicated commercial position for nightlife, late-night bars, clubs and event venues with named customer cases. Festival cashless wristband platforms run their underlying top-up flow on card rails. This is the largest unclaimed position in the vertical.

9. Sub-segments where the model fits

Ranked by structural fit with a fixed-fee, transaction-based model. These are described as structural fit, not as commercial targets.

Strongest fit

  1. Festival and event cashless top-ups. Cashless wristband platforms route top-ups through card rails today. An A2A rail removes card scheme cost from the top-up flow, and one platform relationship reaches many events. Top-up values of EUR 40 to EUR 200 sit comfortably above the fixed-fee break-even against every benchmark.
  2. Premium and VIP venue flows. Transaction values of EUR 500 to EUR 5,000 produce effective fixed-fee rates of 0.01 to 0.10%, well below any card acquirer rate. These add high-value transactions on top of the volume, but are one flow among many rather than the target in themselves.
  3. Mid-tier event ticketing. Where major ticketing platforms do not lock the buyer flow, an A2A rail in a promoter-direct setup is competitive against ticketing fees that run from roughly 7 to 13.5%.

Secondary fit

  1. Business-to-business supplier payments in hospitality. Beverage wholesalers, festival agencies paying acts, venue groups paying suppliers and landlords. High transaction value, low chargeback exposure.
  2. Per-tab venue flows. Where a venue aggregates an evening's spend into one or two transactions of EUR 200 or more, the fixed fee is competitive.

Addressable through the right model

  1. Mainstream nightclubs and late-night bars. Mainstream venues are addressable, and the model is what unlocks the opportunity. The payment captures the guest's full evening of EUR 40 to EUR 80, aggregated into one transaction through a tab or pre-authorisation. That lands at an effective rate near 1% and beats card acquiring across the board. Per-venue revenue is steady and scales: mainstream economics work through distribution that spreads acquisition cost across many venues (point-of-sale integration or a chain or platform relationship). With tab-level capture and a distribution channel, mainstream is firmly in scope.

Where the model is applied selectively

  • Italy. The opportunity is the Sardegna premium pocket at EUR 31 per spectator, which supports a focused premium presence. The EUR 14.40 national mainstream figure is already a whole-visit average, so Italy is approached through its premium destinations.
  • Tab-level capture is the model everywhere. The fixed fee is applied at the level of the tab or top-up rather than the individual drink, which is how the economics work across every market. This is a feature of how the rail is deployed, and it travels with the model.

10. Geographic priority

Markets are ranked in two steps. First, a transaction-value gate: the ranking prioritises markets that clear the average transaction value (ATV) level at which a fixed fee is most competitive. Second, a weighted score across the criteria applied to the qualifying markets.

Step one: the transaction-value gate

The fixed fee becomes competitive against percentage-priced A2A specialists above roughly EUR 67 to EUR 83, and against card acquirers from roughly EUR 15 to EUR 25. The ranking prioritises markets whose mainstream ATV sits in or above this range. Italy mainstream (EUR 14.40 per attendance) is approached through its premium pocket (Sardegna) rather than its mainstream tier, so it is held out of the primary ranking notwithstanding its large absolute size.

11. Conclusion

The market evidence supports moving forward, with a precise definition of where the model fits.

  1. The segment is structurally high risk under the global card acquirer framework, producing elevated processing fees, rolling reserves and chargeback exposure that are documented at the acquirer and regulatory level.
  2. The aggregate market is substantial (EU pubs, bars and coffee shops at EUR 100.6 billion, national venue populations in the tens of thousands), and because the fee is the same on every transaction, the opportunity is volume earned across the whole customer base.
  3. No A2A provider has built a dedicated position in nightlife, late-night bars, clubs and event venues with named customer cases, and festival cashless platforms run their top-up flow on card rails. This is the clearest unclaimed position in the vertical.
  4. The fixed-fee model at approximately EUR 0.40 (Nordics) and EUR 0.50 (wider EU) is cheaper than card acquiring at almost any value above approximately EUR 15 to EUR 25, and cheaper than percentage-priced A2A specialists above roughly EUR 67 to EUR 83. Because the fee is the same on every transaction, the revenue is driven by volume across the whole customer base; the largest pools are festival and event top-ups, mainstream high-frequency venues, mid-tier ticketing and business-to-business supplier payments.
  5. Per-venue payback at a benchmark customer acquisition cost (CAC) of EUR 1,200 to EUR 1,800 is well inside fintech industry norms even at conservative capture. Three-year revenue ranges run from approximately EUR 500,000 (venue-only, conservative) to approximately EUR 25 million (aggressive, with platform relationships), with the platform relationship as the dominant variable.
  6. The structural advantages of A2A payments (no card scheme chargeback, no rolling reserve, fast settlement, a fraud rate of 0.013% against 0.045% for the wider industry) map directly onto the documented costs of the segment, and the strongest of these advantages are realised at the high transaction values that define the qualifying sub-segments.

The evidence is sufficient to proceed from research into a focused commercial phase.

The focus is mainstream nightlife: licensed nightclubs, late-night bars, cocktail venues, music venues, festivals, event venues and adjacent venue-based payment flows.
