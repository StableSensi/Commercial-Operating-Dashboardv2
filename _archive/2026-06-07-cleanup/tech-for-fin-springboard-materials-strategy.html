Tech for Fin springboard — prep notes

Prep notes for Aryze's mentor-programme springboard session: the 15-minute context block, listening priorities for the sparring, standard responses if someone pushes commercial, and the walking-out checklist.

Owner: Rabii Esteitie, Director, Sales & Marketing · 21 May 2026 · Confidential

Session frame

Objective (from agenda): Understand why banks are hesitating to adopt stablecoin infrastructure and the hurdles they face. Posture: Shared learning. Not commercial qualification. Listen more than you speak. Aryze in the room: Rabii Esteitie, Director, Sales & Marketing.

1. 15-minute context block (the only speaking slot)

Use this as scaffolding, not a script. Bullets are pacing markers, not lines to read.

0:00 — 2:00 · Frame the session

  • Thank the room. One sentence on the format: "I have 15 minutes of context, then we open the floor."
  • Who Aryze is: Copenhagen-based fintech building financial infrastructure for institutions.
  • Hard frame: "We are the infrastructure layer. The customer remains issuer and operator under its own licence."
  • What you are not here for: commercial qualification. What you are here for: a read on where the European digital cash space is actually moving.

2:00 — 6:00 · Where Aryze sits

  • Two products are in scope today:
    • Stablecoin Factory. Self-serve, AI-native deployment layer for builders and exploratory teams launching a simple stablecoin. Lowest friction path from concept to live deployment.
    • Digital Cash. Enterprise operating layer for licensed institutions to issue and operate digital cash under their own licence. Settlement, ramps, treasury, multi-currency issuance, interoperability.
  • One sentence on each product. Do not blend them. Two products, two buyers.
  • On-ramps for Digital Cash are delivered through a partnership with Mastercard Open Banking. Name it once, move on.
  • What Aryze does not do: issuer, licensed operator, holder of the customer's licence. The customer keeps that.

6:00 — 13:00 · The three observations

This is the core of the 15 minutes. Roughly two minutes per observation. Land each one, then move.

Observation 1 — Issuance is solved. Operation is the gap.

  • A team can deploy a simple token to a public chain inside a sprint. That part is commodity.
  • What that does not give you: settlement, ramps, treasury, interoperability with existing systems, integration with the core, AML and CTF tooling, observability, exception handling, reconciliation.
  • The interesting problem in 2026 is operational. Not cryptographic.
  • One line to drop in the room: "If you can issue but you cannot operate, you have a press release, not a product."

Observation 2 — Demand is real but uneven.

  • Strongest pull we see: tokenised deposits, intra-bank settlement, corporate treasury, clearing.
  • Weaker: pure retail and consumer-facing digital cash.
  • The market headline of "stablecoin demand" hides this asymmetry. Issuance volume is not the same as institutional pull.
  • Question to get answered back: where is the pull in your institution today, and where is it noise.

Observation 3 — Builders and institutions need different things, often inside the same organisation.

  • Innovation teams want low-friction, self-serve, fast iteration. They want to try.
  • Production teams need full operating infrastructure. They want to run, with controls.
  • Same logo, completely different requirements.
  • That is why Aryze runs two products, not one. It is not market segmentation theatre. The buyers genuinely differ.

13:00 — 15:00 · Hand-off to sparring

  • "I will stop there. There are six questions framed in the pre-read. I am most interested in question 1 (what you actually see today) and question 3 (what keeps institutions on the sidelines). Where do you want to start?"
  • Sit down. Stop talking.

2. Listening priorities (the 55 minutes of sparring)

What to draw out of the room. Pin this next to the notebook.

On demand

  • Concrete demand signals from corporate treasury, not retail.
  • Whether tokenised deposits is actually moving inside any of their institutions, or still a slide-deck conversation.
  • What "ready" looks like for them: regulatory, technical, governance.

On what is blocking them

  • Why their bank has not moved yet. The honest version, not the polished one.
  • What their risk and compliance teams said last time stablecoin infrastructure was pitched.
  • What would have to change for them to move in the next 12 months.

On the stack three years out

  • Who they think owns settlement, custody, issuance, distribution, on-ramps in 2029.
  • Where they think banks lose to specialists, and where banks hold the line.
  • Whether the card networks (Mastercard, Visa) become rails for digital cash or get routed around.

On partnership patterns

  • How they evaluate specialist infrastructure vendors. Signal versus noise criteria.
  • Who they are already partnered with in this space, and what that partnership actually delivers.
  • What separates a credible partner from a vendor they ignore.

On external signals

  • MiCA implementation maturity. What is real, what is paperwork.
  • ECB work on tokenised wholesale settlement and the digital euro track.
  • US developments and whether they shift European bank posture.
  • Asia signals, particularly Hong Kong and Singapore stablecoin regimes.

For the wrap-up

  • The 15-minute wrap-up is the take-home. Be explicit: "Before we close, I want one signal from each of you to share with a peer bank, and one thing Aryze should pay attention to."

3. Standard responses if someone pushes commercial

The session is framed as learning. If a mentor presses, there is a clean exit line each time. Do not improvise these.

"So what would I actually buy from Aryze?"

"Not what we are here for today. Happy to follow up separately. What I would value now is your read on where the operational gap actually bites for an institution like yours."

"Is this a sales pitch?"

"No. The room is here to give Aryze a read on the European digital cash space. If it tips into sales, please push back and I will reset."

"What is your traction? Customers, pipeline, ARR?"

"Honest answer: we have early customers on Stablecoin Factory and ongoing institutional conversations on Digital Cash. I am here to learn, not to qualify. Happy to share specifics in a follow-up."

"Why should banks trust Aryze specifically?"

"That is not for me to answer in this room. I would rather hear what would make any infrastructure partner credible for you. That is question 4 in the pre-read."

"Are you regulated?"

"Aryze is infrastructure. The customer remains issuer and operator under its own licence. We are not the regulated party in the chain."

"Can you do X?" (off-ramps as standalone, recurring under PSD2, payouts as a standalone flow, retail consumer products)

"Not productised at Aryze today. Let me come back to you with what is possible."

4. Things to avoid

  • Mixing Stablecoin Factory and Digital Cash in one sentence. Two products. Two buyers. Two narratives.
  • Promising capability Aryze does not have today. Off-ramps standalone, recurring under PSD2, retail consumer products.
  • Industry buzzwords. No "revolutionising", no "future of money", no "seamless", no "game-changing", no "next-generation".
  • Pitching the Mastercard partnership beyond what it is. It is the on-ramp partnership for Digital Cash. That is the sentence.
  • Defending the thesis when it gets pushed on. The whole point is to let the room test it.
  • Numbers you cannot source. No "110+ clients" from the Mastercard deck. No conversion uplift figures. No chargeback rates.
  • Speaking past the 15 minutes. The clock is the clock. Sit down on time.

5. Walking-out checklist

By the time you leave the room, you should have:

  • Six to eight named signals from mentors on where they see real digital cash demand inside their institutions.
  • An honest read on whether "operation is the gap" survives contact with senior bank operators.
  • Two or three concrete partnership patterns that work for banks and specialist vendors.
  • One or two mentors who could later become intro-givers or door-openers. Note who, and why.
  • A short list of regulatory or market signals the room is watching that you were not.

Write this up the same day. Twenty minutes, max. Otherwise it evaporates.
