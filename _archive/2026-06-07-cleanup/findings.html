Nightlife findings memo

The verified evidence base — market size, the high-risk card classification and its cost, the chargeback and fraud environment, transaction values, the solution landscape and the known gaps.

Owner: Commercial · 30 May 2026 · Confidential

↩ Package overview Source .md

Date: 30 May 2026

Scope: Mainstream nightlife, bars, clubs, festivals and events across the Nordics, the UK and the EU. Adult, pornographic and strip-club businesses are excluded.

Confidence key

Verified (confirmed against a primary or industry source), indicative (well supported, secondary), single-source (one source only), estimate (calculated, not observed), not available (could not be sourced).

Sources

The findings draw on the raw research in 01-Findings/Sources/: the nightlife and events viability deep research, the cross-market average transaction value deep research, the cross-vertical analysis, the Danish market research, and the Danish Phase 1 sizing. Headline figures were re-checked against public sources.

1. Market size and structure

  • The European pubs, bars and coffee shops sector was EUR 100.6 billion in 2025, growing at a 5.8% five-year compound rate, with a marginal 0.1% dip in 2025 (IBISWorld, verified).
  • The UK nightclub population is 823, down 36% since March 2020. Late-night venues of all types number 2,424, down 26.4% since March 2020. UK nightlife contracted 4.1% in 2025 (NTIA, verified).
  • Germany has 33,911 bars and nightclubs enterprises in 2025 (IBISWorld, verified).
  • Italy has 2,500 dance venues with 34 million visits and EUR 2 billion turnover (EUR 500 million direct) in 2024 (SILB-FIPE, verified).
  • Spain pubs, bars and nightclubs revenue was above EUR 20 billion in 2024. The broader beverage-serving population is around 164,000 venues, of which nightlife is a subset; a nightlife-specific count is not separately published (Statista, IBISWorld; revenue verified, nightlife-specific count not available).
  • Denmark cafes, bars and discotheques (NACE 563000) generated DKK 6.9 billion in 2024 (eStatistik, verified). Approximately 350 DRC NAT member venues (estimate).
  • France has approximately 1,500 discotheques, a sector in decline, with 430 closures reported since March 2025 (SNDLL, single-source).
  • The market is highly fragmented on the continent and more consolidated in the UK and the Nordics (indicative).

2. High-risk merchant classification and its cost

  • Nightlife venues operate under merchant category code (MCC) 5813, classified by acquirers as medium to high risk across Europe (acquirer underwriting guides: PXP, Rapyd, eflow, Corepay; verified).
  • High-risk card acquiring runs 3.5 to 7% all-in, with rolling reserves of 5 to 15% held 90 to 180 days (international acquirer guides; indicative, not a Denmark-specific measurement).
  • Danish acquirers publish maximum rates by law. Foreign-card ceilings are far above domestic, for example Nets up to 6.26% plus DKK 0.19 (mobiletransaction.org, compiling legally published rates; single-source). The structural point, that foreign cards are uncapped in the EEA and cost materially more, is verified; the exact per-acquirer cells are single-source.
  • Danish card fees are rising. Nets' Dankort revenue cap for physical trade rose 18.8% for 2026 (KFST, verified).

3. Chargeback and fraud environment

  • The Visa Acquirer Monitoring Program (VAMP) merchant Excessive threshold dropped to 1.5% on 1 April 2026, with a USD 8 per-dispute fee and no warning tier (US, Canada, EU) (Visa, Merchant Risk Council; verified).
  • Mastercard Excessive Chargeback Merchant (ECM) thresholds are 100 or more chargebacks plus a 1.5% ratio. The MATCH list is a five-year industry blacklist (verified).
  • In Denmark a consumer can dispute a card payment for up to 120 days, and up to 13 months for alleged misuse, with the bank refunding by the end of the next business day (Finanstilsynet, Forbrugerombudsmanden; verified).
  • Account-to-account (A2A) payment fraud ran at 0.013% by volume against 0.045% for the wider industry in H1 2025, rising to 0.023% by September 2025. Authorised push payment fraud is 74% of open banking fraud cases (Open Banking Limited, December 2025; verified).
  • Two UK event-sector failures show concentrated chargeback exposure: Festicket entered administration owing GBP 22.5 million and Pollen collapsed owing GBP 78.6 million, both 2022 (IQ Magazine, Mixmag, BBC; verified).
  • A per-merchant chargeback rate specifically for MCC 5813 is not disclosed by acquirers or trade bodies (not available).

4. Average transaction value

  • Verified primary points: Madrid at EUR 79.10 per night out (Spain Nightlife, 2025), and Italy national mainstream at EUR 14.40 per attendance, derived from SILB-FIPE direct revenue divided by attendances (verified).
  • Festival average daily spend per attendee is EUR 81 (Weezevent 2024 barometer, 800 events, 20 million-plus participants), composed of EUR 48 tickets, EUR 18.41 food, EUR 9.75 bar, EUR 4.47 other (verified).
  • Other market ranges (UK GBP 30 to 50 mainstream, Stockholm SEK 800 to 1,200, Berlin EUR 50 to 80, Ibiza EUR 150 plus, and so on) come from local nightlife guides (indicative).
  • The pattern across markets is a barbell: many low-value drink transactions and a smaller number of high-value premium and VIP transactions (indicative).

5. Existing solution landscape

  • A2A providers are established in adjacent service industries (restaurants, hotels, ticketing) but no provider has built a dedicated position in nightlife, late-night bars, clubs and event venues with named customer cases (strong indication, based on the absence of published cases).
  • Festival cashless wristband platforms (Tappit, Weezevent, Intellitix, Glownet and others) run their underlying top-up flow on card rails (indicative).
  • The festival cashless platforms market was USD 2.12 billion in 2024 and is forecast at USD 9.89 billion by 2033, a 16.5% compound rate (Growth Market Reports, verified).
  • Competitor scale: Atoa is early-stage (pre-seed USD 2.2 million), UK-only; Noda spans roughly 2,000 banks across 28 countries; Yoello was acquired by Epos Now in December 2024; Finexer offers 99% UK coverage as white-label infrastructure (verified).
  • Aryze runs Pay by Bank on Mastercard Open Banking, connected to around 2,900 European banks (verified).
  • In Denmark, NightPay is a loyalty and top-up wallet owned by the Rekom group, running on card rails across roughly 75 to 80 venues, and was challenged by the consumer ombudsman on electronic-money grounds in April 2026 (DK research; single-source).

6. Behavioural and adoption signals

  • Mainstream consumers in Denmark are already accustomed to phone-based payment, so a bank-app payment is no harder than what they do daily (indicative).
  • The affluent top end (VIP and bottle service) tends to pay by card, often for rewards, and is less likely to switch payment method (indicative).
  • Nightlife is a relationship-driven industry where warm introductions carry more weight than cold outreach (indicative).

7. What we do not know (gaps)

  • Per-venue annual transaction counts (not available).
  • Per-merchant chargeback rate for MCC 5813 (not available).
  • Actual negotiated acquirer rates and rolling reserves at venue level (not available).
  • Share of foreign-card transactions in tourist-heavy venues (not available).
  • Whether Danish nightclubs are actually placed in a high-risk pricing tier or receive standard pricing (not available).
  • Festival platforms' actual underlying rails per contract (not available).
  • Nightlife-specific A2A penetration per country (not available).
  • Confirmation of the France active-venue count beyond the single trade-body source (not available).

These gaps can only be closed by primary operator data or a pilot, not by further desk research.
