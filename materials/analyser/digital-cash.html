Part 1 — The customer

Definition

Digital Cash is enterprise operating infrastructure — the opposite end of the spectrum from Stablecoin Factory's self-serve deployment layer. The institution remains issuer and operator under its own licence; Aryze delivers the infrastructure behind it.

This customer is not after a demo or a pilot with no execution path. They have an institutional need, a budget that can fund an infrastructure engagement, and a buying committee that spans treasury, legal, compliance, risk and technology.

Strongest institution types, in order

What the customer wants

"Operate digital cash under our own licence, with full institutional infrastructure behind it."

The institution wants control, credibility and operational depth — not speed alone. Issuance by itself does not solve settlement, liquidity, treasury, ramps, compliance, auditability, interoperability, reporting or integration with existing systems. Institutions that get serious about digital cash hit a wall between ambition and operational readiness. Digital Cash is the layer that closes that gap.

The pains we solve

  1. Issuance is not operation. Simple issuance solves none of settlement, liquidity, treasury, ramps, compliance, auditability, interoperability, reporting or integration.
  2. An internal build is slow, expensive and complex. Typically 12–24 months and five or more full-time engineers for a serious in-house stack.
  3. Governance stakeholders demand a credible operating model. Pilots without an operating story do not pass the risk committee; treasury will not move money onto a proof of concept.
  4. Integration with existing systems is hard. Core banking, treasury management, ledger engines and wallets are legacy infrastructure that must be respected.
  5. They want control without building everything. The classic build-vs-buy tension at institutional scale.
  6. Regulator readiness. The institution must be able to answer "how do you operate this?"
  7. Multi-party complexity. Settlement involves multiple counterparties; interoperability and standards conformance are not optional.

Fit & walk-away signals

Strong-fit signals — the more that are present, the better the fit:

  • Licensed, with the licence covering digital cash or related activity
  • A concrete use case — tokenised deposits, intra-bank settlement, treasury, multi-currency liquidity, RWA settlement
  • Internal maturity to engage all functions at once
  • Existing infrastructure to integrate, not replace
  • Budget framed as capex/opex, not a pilot
  • An executive sponsor identified, or a path to one
  • A multi-year horizon — they do not expect to be live in six weeks
  • A working regulator relationship
  • Counterparties and an ecosystem that can support settlement

Walk-away signals — when these are true, it is not a fit:

  • Unlicensed buyer — outside the fit, and a candidate for re-routing or disqualification
  • Wants Aryze as issuer or regulated operator — not the product
  • Only needs simple stablecoin deployment — a fit for Stablecoin Factory
  • Pure research mode, without an execution path or budget
  • Cannot support legal, compliance and risk review
  • No executive sponsor and no path to one
  • Wants a product demo out of curiosity, with no operating need
  • Expects deployment in weeks — wrong scale for institutional infrastructure
  • In conflict with their regulator — Aryze cannot solve an existing compliance issue
  • Wants everything custom-built from scratch — Aryze is infrastructure, not a consulting build
Qualification depth — best-fit profile, buying triggers, who buys & first-call questions

Best-fit institution profile

  • Already licensed — and the licence covers digital cash issuance or related activity
  • A concrete use case — not "we are exploring tokenised deposits" but "we must settle intra-bank transactions with tokens before end of 2026"
  • The internal maturity to engage treasury, legal, compliance, risk and technology at the same time
  • Existing infrastructure that must be integrated, not replaced — core banking, treasury management, ledger, wallets
  • Budget framing as a capex/opex line, not a pilot budget
  • An identified executive sponsor — typically board-adjacent
  • Able to carry a 6–18 month sales and implementation cycle without losing interest

Buying triggers

  • A compliance deadline — regulators requiring settlement modernisation, or MiCA pressure on the operating model
  • A peer move — a peer bank announces tokenised deposits, and the institution must follow or fall behind
  • A validated use case — the pilot phase is over, now it must go live
  • The cost of existing settlement rails — correspondent fees, T+2 settlement cost, FX cost
  • Liquidity pressure — treasury wants faster settlement to free up capital
  • Strategic positioning — the board or executive team has put digital cash on the roadmap
  • Regulator consultation — invited into regulatory dialogue and needing to show capability
  • Acquisition preparation — positioning as a strategic asset

Who buys

The buying committee is much wider than a merchant sale. Primary: the executive sponsor (board-adjacent — CFO, COO or Chief Digital Officer; the role matters early, since deals do not close without an internal champion at executive level), Head of Treasury, Head of Transaction Banking, Head of Payments, the Digital Assets lead (usually programme owner), CFO and COO. Secondary but decisive: CIO/CTO on integration, the Chief Compliance Officer / MLRO on regulatory framing, General Counsel on legal structure, and the risk committee.

The executive sponsor is the pivotal role. Without one identified, the engagement tends to remain an open-ended discussion rather than a deal.

Qualification questions for the first institutional conversation

  1. Licence status: which licence do you operate under, and what does it cover for digital cash, tokenisation or settlement?
  2. Use case: tokenised deposits, settlement, treasury, multi-currency, RWA settlement, or something else?
  3. Trigger: why is this a 2026 conversation rather than 2025 or 2027?
  4. Executive sponsor: who is it internally, and who from treasury, legal, compliance, risk and technology will be in the room?
  5. Existing infrastructure: what must this integrate with?
  6. Governance and audit: what requirements does your institution put on a programme like this?
  7. Time horizon: pilot, phased, or full operation?
  8. Budget framing: funded, or still building the business case?

Questions #1, #2, #4 and #7 — licence status, use case, executive sponsor and time horizon — are the ones a concrete proposal depends on.

How we position it

"Enterprise infrastructure for already-licensed institutions that want to operate digital cash under their own licence, with the full operating layer — settlement, ramps, treasury, multi-currency, interoperability — needed to move from issuance to live operation."

The positioning centres on control, credibility and infrastructure depth, in institutional rather than Web3 terms. This customer needs institutional operating infrastructure, not a stablecoin tool.

Market context & the competitive field

Market context

  • MiCA is fully operational — EU institutions must position under the new framework
  • The EU Digital Euro project gives institutional digital cash legitimacy and attention
  • Tier-1 banks have launched tokenised deposits (JPM Coin, Citi Token Services, BBVA, Santander) — pressure on tier-2/3 banks to follow
  • Wholesale CBDC pilots run in the UK (BoE), EU (ECB) and Switzerland (SNB Project Helvetia)
  • DLT-based settlement is gaining acceptance — Project Agora (BIS) and Project mBridge are no longer just research
  • Cross-border correspondent banking still costs 5–8% in many corridors — the incentive for DLT settlement is real
  • Tokenised T-bills and money-market funds (BlackRock BUIDL, Franklin OnChain, Ondo) create settlement needs for institutional liquidity flows

Digital Cash occupies the space those projects leave open: operating depth under the institution's own licence without consortium lock-in, Mastercard on-ramp integration, and a migration path from Stablecoin Factory where relevant.

Part 2 — The value proposition

The institution's jobs

Functional

Issue digital cash under their own licence and governance; run programmes that meet treasury, settlement, liquidity or value-movement needs; settle transactions on-ledger with credible operational backing; manage liquidity and reserves on a token basis; operate on/off-ramps to traditional payments; integrate with existing core banking, treasury management, ledger and wallet systems; move assets across networks and counterparties; issue in multiple currencies.

Professional

Satisfy internal stakeholders — legal, compliance, risk, treasury, technology — with credibility and operational depth; stand behind the programme in front of regulators, the board and the risk committee; demonstrate institutional capability to peer banks and counterparties.

Emotional

Confidence that the programme stands up to regulator scrutiny; trust that the infrastructure provider is not a single point of failure; pride in a credible operating model rather than a proof of concept; control — it is the institution's programme, not Aryze's.

Part 3 — The product

Overview

Digital Cash is built for institutions that need more than simple issuance. These customers are not looking for a token demo — they need the operating infrastructure required to make digital cash work in real financial environments: settlement, on/off-ramps, treasury functionality, multi-currency issuance, interoperability, and asset movement across environments.

The positioning is precise: Aryze delivers infrastructure. The customer remains issuer and operator under their own licence. That distinction is central. Digital Cash is not the product for customers who only need a lightweight stablecoin test — that is Stablecoin Factory.

Enterprise operating infrastructure for already-licensed institutions to issue and operate digital cash under their own licence, governance and control.

What's in the box

In the box — confirmed

  • Issuance — under the institution's own licence; Aryze is not the issuer.
  • Settlement — supporting clearing and settlement between participants.
  • On/off-ramps — between digital cash and traditional payments, via the Mastercard Open Banking partnership.
  • Treasury functionality — liquidity management and reserve handling.
  • Multi-currency issuance — several currencies on the same operating layer.
  • Interoperability & asset movement — moving assets across environments (private and public DLT), networks and counterparties.
  • Governance hooks — controls, approvals and audit trails matching the institution's governance model.
  • Reporting & integration — with existing core banking, treasury management, ledger and wallet systems.
  • Operations — enterprise SLA, 24/7 institutional-grade support, a multi-year partnership model, standards conformance (ISO 20022, FATF, MiCA where relevant), and built-in AML/KYC/sanctions-screening hooks.

Deliberately not in the box

  • Aryze does not hold the customer's licence and takes on no regulated activity.
  • Aryze does not become the issuer — the institution issues.
  • Aryze does not operate the programme — the institution operates under its own licence.
  • No "live in six weeks" — the cycle is 6–18 months, and speed is not the proposition.
  • No "plug-and-play" — the term does not hold up with an institutional buying committee.
  • No consortium building — that is Fnality/Partior territory; Aryze serves single institutions.
  • No central-bank functions and no licence delivery.
  • Specific networks, jurisdictions and custody partners are confirmed per deal.

How it is sold

An enterprise sale: long cycle, wide committee, high consideration. Channels: direct institutional outreach, warm introductions via advisors, lawyers, regulators and industry bodies, institutional events (Sibos, Money20/20, Point Zero, Digital Assets Week), strategic partnerships, inbound RFPs and regulator-driven introductions.

The typical path: multi-touch discovery (surface the use case, confirm the licence, identify the executive sponsor) → a workshop with treasury, compliance and technology in the room → solution architecture against the existing stack → commercial framing with phased deployment → legal, risk and procurement (expect months) → pilot or phase-1 deployment → live operation and expansion. 6–18 months is normal, and speed is not the proposition.

How Digital Cash is described

  • The language used: Aryze as the infrastructure provider; regulatory complexity acknowledged rather than waved away; operational, institutional terms — settlement, liquidity, governance, auditability — in place of Web3 jargon.
  • The language avoided: "we'll issue the digital cash for you" · "Aryze becomes your operator" · "live in weeks" · "plug-and-play" · "stablecoin" used loosely — institutions can dislike the term, and the customer's own language tends to prevail: digital cash, tokenised deposits, settlement asset.

Pricing

Priced as an enterprise infrastructure engagement, depending on scope (settlement only / treasury / multi-currency / full), volume framing, integration depth and the multi-year commitment. A number is not quoted before the use case and integration have been scoped; pricing is framed once that scope is understood.
