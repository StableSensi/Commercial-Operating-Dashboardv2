Part 1 — The customer

Who it's for

The ideal Pay by Bank customer is a UK or European merchant or payment-driven platform where incoming customer payments directly affect margin, settlement speed, cash flow, customer experience, fraud exposure, reconciliation or daily operations.

Pay by Bank covers the customer → merchant direction only — checkout, deposits, top-ups, instant customer funding. If the customer mainly needs outgoing payments (player payouts, or refunds as a standalone flow), it is not an Aryze fit today.

This customer is not looking for another checkout option. They have a real payment problem that costs them money, time or customers.

Where it runs

  • Scope: UK + EU/EEA merchants. Mastercard Open Banking rails cover 19 European markets and more than 2,900 banks.
  • Staged rollout: UK is live via the Volt.io partnership. The EU/EEA rollout begins late Q2 2026, pending the Volt-extension contract. Until then, EU prospects are pipeline conversations — not commit-to-go-live timelines.
  • Bank depth varies by country — confirmed per customer, never guessed.

The five pains we solve

  1. High payment costs — card fees scale with the amount. Scheme fees have risen 75%+ since 2021 for large merchants.
  2. Slow settlement — money is not available in time to support cash flow, liquidity or operations.
  3. Operational friction — refunds, reporting, reconciliation and payment-status tracking create work for finance and ops.
  4. Chargeback and dispute burden — iGaming runs 2–4% chargeback rates against 0.5–1% in normal e-commerce. Cost per dispute: EUR 20–100, with a loaded cost around 2.5× the transaction value.
  5. Lack of control — insufficient visibility across the payment flow from initiation to settlement.

What the customer wants

"Get paid faster, with less friction and more control."

This customer does not want a future-of-money story. They want a better payment operating model: lower cost, faster settlement, less dispute burden, cleaner reconciliation, and better control over incoming funds.

Fit & walk-away signals

Strong-fit signals — the more that are present, the better the fit:

  • Payment performance discussed at leadership level
  • Card fees visible in the payment P&L
  • Chargebacks, fraud or disputes operationally relevant
  • Settlement speed has real cash-flow impact
  • There is an internal payment owner
  • Payment operations already painful or strategically important
  • Actively looking for alternatives to card rails
  • Deposits, top-ups or customer-funding flows commercially important
  • Technical capacity to integrate a new rail

Walk-away signals — when these are true, it is not a fit:

  • Operates outside the UK + EU/EEA
  • Payment volume low or low-priority
  • Card costs not a meaningful issue
  • Settlement speed does not matter to them
  • They just want "more payment methods"
  • No internal payment owner
  • Cannot support an integration
  • No clear pain around cost, settlement, reconciliation, chargebacks or control
  • Primary need is outgoing payments (payouts, withdrawals, standalone refunds) — we cover the incoming direction only. If the use case can be split, we take the deposit side now and flag payouts as open.
  • Wants AIS features (account aggregation, balance checks, account data) — we are PIS-only, no AIS at all
  • Wants standalone recurring payments — only possible via Mastercard Payment Services DK/NO until PSD3 (estimated 2027–28)

Buying triggers

A prospect becomes a strong fit when at least one of these is active:

  • Card costs are too high and eat margin
  • Settlement is too slow for cash flow
  • Chargebacks and disputes create financial and operational burden
  • Refund handling is painful
  • Reconciliation creates too much manual work
  • Payment status visibility is weak
  • The current payment flow is too indirect to control
  • The business needs instant or near-instant customer funding (deposits, top-ups)
  • Card flows fit the customer journey poorly
  • Payment performance has been discussed at leadership level — the strongest signal of all

Qualification questions for the first call

  1. How many transactions a month today? Average ticket?
  2. What total card fee % do you pay (acquiring + scheme + interchange)? How much has it risen since 2021?
  3. Where is your chargeback rate today? Do you feel pressure from the VAMP 1.5% threshold from April 2026?
  4. Which markets are critical — UK only, or specific EU countries?
  5. Which PSP / payment stack do you run today? Do you already have an open-banking provider (Trustly, TrueLayer, Volt, Token, Banked)?
  6. What ranks highest among your pains: cost, chargebacks, conversion, settlement speed, or operations/reconciliation?
  7. Who joins the decision — Payments, Finance, Risk, Tech, Compliance?
  8. Do you have the engineering capacity and timeline to integrate a new rail?

Volume, card cost and the current payment stack — questions one, two and five — most shape whether there is a concrete proposal to make.

Part 3 — The product

Overview

Pay by Bank lets a business receive incoming customer payments through Open Banking payment flows on Mastercard's rails instead of traditional card rails. The customer approves the payment directly in their own bank (strong customer authentication), and the money moves bank to bank — not through a card scheme.

Flow direction — important: Pay by Bank covers customer → merchant only: checkout, deposits, top-ups, instant customer funding. The opposite direction (payouts, withdrawals, standalone refunds) is not productised at Aryze.

For the merchant the value is not another checkout option. The value is a cleaner, faster, more controllable payment flow.

What's in the box

In the box — confirmed

  • A2A payments on Mastercard rails — customer → merchant: checkout, deposits, top-ups. The customer approves directly in their bank.
  • Fixed fee per transaction — our main argument against cards: the same price regardless of amount.
  • Near-zero chargebacks — A2A is irrevocable and SCA-approved in the customer's bank. No scheme dispute path.
  • RememberMe — a token valid for 15 months. Repeat depositors approve in roughly half the clicks of a first deposit — a direct conversion gain on repeat flows.
  • Theming / custom branding — logo, colours and font at checkout, so the merchant's brand carries through.
  • Structured onboarding — map the flow → KYB → integration & test → go live, with callbacks, reporting and transaction monitoring built in.
  • Platform reliability — 99.99%+ uptime on the Mastercard Open Banking platform: geo-redundancy, automatic failover, 24/7 support.

Deliberately not in the box

  • AIS features — account owner verification, balance checks, account aggregation, spending insights, KYC via open banking, combined AIS+PIS flows.
  • Payouts (merchant → customer) — withdrawals and standalone refunds are not live at Aryze. If a customer asks: we cover the incoming direction today; outgoing is on the roadmap.
  • Standalone recurring — not possible under PSD2; only via Mastercard Payment Services DK/NO. Standalone expected with PSD3 (estimated 2027–28).
  • Refunds — always check with product before promising refund flows.

Aryze operates PIS only, on the customer → merchant direction. The items on the right exist on Mastercard's platform or on a roadmap, but are not productised at Aryze.

Where a customer references a Mastercard feature that Aryze has not productised, it is named as open rather than implied as available.

How it is sold

A commercial, merchant-focused motion: direct merchant conversations, warm introductions, payment-focused partnerships, the website and savings calculator, LinkedIn, targeted outreach, and product demos with sandbox access. The buying group typically spans finance (cost and cash flow), payments and product (the payment journey), risk and fraud (disputes and chargebacks), operations (reconciliation and reporting) and engineering (the integration). The primary buyer is the Payments Operations Lead; secondary buyers are the Head of Payments, CFO and COO. The product is sold on payment performance — not technical novelty.

Pricing

Fixed fee per transaction — the opposite of card rails, which scale with the amount. The concrete price depends on volume, markets and scope. The savings calculator produces an estimate on a customer's own numbers, set against their current setup.
