Part 1 — The customer

Definition

Stablecoin Factory is infrastructure, not regulated operation. The customer remains the issuer; Aryze delivers the deployment layer. If the customer needs a full institutional operating model from day one — settlement, ramps, treasury, multi-currency, governance — that is Digital Cash, not Stablecoin Factory.

This customer is not after a demo. They have a use case they want to test in practice, and they want to reach live deployment without burning months on a custom build or enterprise contracts.

Strongest customer types, in order

What the customer wants

"Launch a simple stablecoin (or tokenised asset) faster, with less friction."

A practical route from concept to live. They want to test a use case before committing to a broader infrastructure model. They value speed, clarity and real-world learning.

The five pains we solve

  1. The path from idea to live is too heavy. Too many technical decisions, components, vendors, legal questions and internal discussions. Teams spend months scoping before testing anything real.
  2. A custom build costs too much. 6–12 months and several full-time engineers, with no guarantee the use case validates.
  3. A fragmented vendor stack. Wallet, ledger, custody, ramps, compliance — all separate suppliers in a typical DIY deployment. Every integration costs time.
  4. Enterprise complexity arrives too early. Teams do not need a full institutional layer to test a use case — but the market pushes them toward it anyway.
  5. No clear starting point. Without a visible deployment flow, stablecoin and RWA projects sit in concept mode for quarters.

Fit & walk-away signals

Strong-fit signals — the more that are present, the better the fit:

  • The use case fits in one sentence
  • A technical team can evaluate via sandbox and documentation
  • The path to live has a time horizon — weeks to a quarter
  • Speed and usability are prioritised over enterprise features
  • The issuer model is clear — the customer or their parent entity holds the licence
  • Self-serve fits — they do not want a six-month enterprise engagement
  • A later move up to Digital Cash is acceptable, not a deal-breaker
  • The motion fit is identified — own programme, partner-led or white-label

Walk-away signals — when these are true, it is not a fit:

  • Needs the full institutional operating layer from day one — route to Digital Cash
  • No concrete use case — only "exploring stablecoins / RWAs"
  • No technical team to use the sandbox
  • Expects Aryze to be the issuer or regulated operator — that is not the product
  • Wants a custom-built enterprise programme from day one
  • Pure research mode, without an execution path or budget
  • Expects a custom contract and a long legal process — that is Digital Cash, not Factory
  • The use case requires a licence the customer does not hold — Aryze does not deliver licences

Who buys

Primary (technically led): Founder / co-founder (especially seed–Series A), CTO / VP Engineering, technical lead or principal architect, Head of Innovation. Secondary (product-led): product lead, Head of Digital Assets, platform lead for partner and white-label motions.

The buying committee is smaller and more technical than Digital Cash — typically 2–4 people, and without treasury, MLRO, compliance or general counsel, because Factory does not touch the institutional setup. Each role weighs the product differently: a CTO focuses on the technical walkthrough and sandbox; a founder focuses on time-to-live and deployment cases; an innovation lead focuses on the pilot framework and success criteria.

Qualification questions for the first call

  1. What is the concrete use case — stablecoin (what kind), tokenised RWA, settlement asset, internal value transfer?
  2. Where are you today — concept, prototype, ready to deploy, or live elsewhere?
  3. What is the trigger now — why this quarter and not next year?
  4. Who is on the team — founder + tech, product, internal venture, innovation?
  5. Who is the issuer — you, or a partner / parent licensed entity?
  6. Which motion fits — own programme, partner-led, or white-label?
  7. What is the time horizon — weeks, a quarter, or longer?
  8. Have you evaluated other deployment paths — custom build, other vendors?
The single most important qualifier is whether the customer needs simple deployment or full institutional operation. Simple deployment is Stablecoin Factory. Full institutional operation is Digital Cash. The two are not interchangeable, and the distinction determines which product applies.

How we position it

"A faster way to launch a simple stablecoin or tokenised asset — self-serve deployment infrastructure that gets you from concept to live without enterprise complexity on day one."

The positioning stays close to speed, simplicity and the deployment path. There is no abstract Web3 story — this customer needs to get from concept to live, not a new financial paradigm.

Market context & the competitive field

Market context

  • The EU MiCA framework is mature — stablecoin deployment under EU regulation is possible, but teams need infrastructure that respects the rules without becoming enterprise-complex on day one
  • US stablecoin legislation is pushing US fintechs to clarify their stablecoin strategy
  • The RWA segment is growing fast — tokenised T-bills, real estate and private credit attract capital; builders want infrastructure that supports RWA deployment without building everything themselves
  • Web3 builders are moving toward real infrastructure after years of disappointing token projects
  • The enterprise pilot phase is over — large banks (BBVA, Santander, JPM, Citi) have run pilots; the mid-market follows
  • Self-serve, AI-assisted tooling is the expectation — not custom code from 2018

Part 3 — The product

Overview

Stablecoin Factory is built for teams that want to move from concept to live deployment without a long enterprise process, a large custom build, or the full complexity of institutional digital-cash infrastructure. It helps teams launch, test and learn faster — a practical way to start before they know whether the use case needs a deeper operating model.

Two distinctions hold throughout: Factory is simple deployment, and Digital Cash is institutional operation. And Factory is infrastructure — Aryze does not become issuer or regulated operator; that role stays with the customer or a partner / parent licensed entity.

A self-serve infrastructure layer that lets teams launch a simple stablecoin or tokenised asset without starting with enterprise complexity on day one.

What's in the box

In the box — confirmed

  • No-code token creation — pick a name, ticker and image; Aryze handles the contract, deploy and verification.
  • Live in about 60 seconds — connect a wallet, complete a short form, confirm, and the token is on-chain.
  • An issuance path for a simple stablecoin or tokenised asset.
  • Multi-chain on EVM networks — Ethereum, Arbitrum, Polygon, BNB Chain and more, with the same token address across chains.
  • LayerZero V2, native — omnichain messaging wired in, so holders can bridge the token across supported networks from day one.
  • Security by default — pausing, allow-lists and audited upgrades; production-grade and tested on mainnet.
  • Token management — issue on demand; send, receive and swap; allow-list trusted users; pause transfers; upgrade securely over time.
  • Documentation and sandbox for technical evaluation without a sales process.
  • Three go-to-market motions (below).
  • A path up to Digital Cash if the use case scales, with the issuer model staying with the customer.

Deliberately not in the box

  • Aryze does not become the issuer — the customer remains issuer under their own licence (or a partner / parent licensed entity).
  • Aryze does not become the regulated operator — the customer operates.
  • Factory is not Digital Cash — full institutional operation routes to Digital Cash.
  • No custom enterprise programmes from day one.
  • RWA capabilities beyond the one-pagers — the product anchors in stablecoins, and RWA capabilities are confirmed against the product rather than assumed.
  • Specific networks, jurisdictions and custody partners — confirmed per deal against the product.
  • The Factory → Digital Cash migration — a path, not a productised step; the technical migration depends on the use case.
  • Licence delivery — Aryze does not deliver the licence.

How it is sold

A product-led and ecosystem-led motion — different from both Pay by Bank (commercial merchant motion) and Digital Cash (enterprise institutional motion). Channels: the self-serve product funnel, developer communities, digital-asset events (Token2049, Permissionless, EthCC, RWA events), technical walkthroughs and demos, founder-led conversations, stablecoin/RWA content, and partner channels for the partner-led and white-label motions.

The typical path: inbound or a warm introduction → a qualifying call (use case, motion fit, technical maturity) → technical walkthrough or sandbox access → solution scoping → commercial framing per motion → deployment → and, when relevant, the path to scale into Digital Cash.

Pricing

Self-serve token creation is free to use. Beyond that, pricing varies per motion — own programme, partner-led and white-label have different economics, commitments and success criteria — and follows the motion fit and scope rather than a quote up front.
