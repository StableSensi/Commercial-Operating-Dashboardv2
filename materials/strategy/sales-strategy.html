The strategy in one line

Three lead sources, six phases, one principle — senior time goes where deals close. How the department turns market activity into signed merchant agreements.

What this is

Three products. Three buyers. Three motions. The strategy below sets out who Aryze sells to, how leads are qualified, where they come from, and how the pipeline runs. It ends where the Sales-to-Onboarding Process begins, at commercial commitment.

Operating principles.

Focus — right to win

Concentrate on accounts where payment economics, deployment speed, licensing position or operating-model depth create a real reason to engage now.

Method — one motion per product

Pay by Bank, Stablecoin Factory and Digital Cash each need a different conversation, cycle and proof point. Mixing them is the most common way deals are lost.

Discipline — visible next steps

Product path, decision owner and next milestone must be documented before management treats an opportunity as real.

Fit before volume.

Demand can come from many channels. Every signal passes the same standard: product fit, buyer intent, commercial value, and a clear next decision.

The anti-pattern: broad selling, loose ownership

  • Generic pitch
  • Unqualified interest
  • Loose follow-up
  • Late risk review
  • The result is activity, but no reliable view of fit or commercial commitment.

The approach: segment-led, decision-disciplined

  • Defined ICP
  • Right motion
  • Qualified meeting
  • Commercial commitment
  • Every opportunity routes by product fit, buyer maturity and the next decision required.

Three products, three motions.

Each product has its own ICP, buyer, cycle, proof point and priority market. The briefs below set out the preparation behind every commercial conversation.

Pay by Bank

High-risk merchants with payment-driven pain

High-risk merchants — primarily iGaming, casino and betting, secondarily trading and other card-under-served high-risk categories — with 100k+ transactions/month and 35+ EUR average ticket, where card cost or chargeback exposure hits margin. Full profile: the Pay by Bank ICP.

Stablecoin Factory

Builders and innovation teams with a concrete use case

Founders, CTOs, product leads and innovation teams who want to deploy a simple stablecoin through a self-serve flow without enterprise complexity on day one.

Digital Cash

Already-licensed institutions ready to operate

Banks, licensed financial institutions, fintech clusters and licensed conglomerates with a concrete settlement, treasury or liquidity use case.

Pay by Bank — high-risk merchants with payment-driven pain

  • Geography: UK live (Volt.io). EU/EEA rollout begins late Q2 2026, pending Volt-extension contract signature.
  • Priority markets: UK gaming, EU iGaming, trading, VAMP-exposed merchants.
  • Anti-ICP: outside UK+EU. Needs payouts or AIS.
  • Motion: commercial-led. Demo plus savings calculator on the customer's own numbers.
  • Cycle: 6–12 weeks SMB. 3–6 months enterprise.
Real example: Tesco Bank uses Pay by Bank for credit-card payments. ~40% volume share. ~25% rolling 12-month growth (Mastercard case).

Stablecoin Factory — builders and innovation teams with a concrete use case

  • Stage: Seed–Series A founders. Internal venture for enterprises.
  • Priority markets: builders with validated use case. Innovation teams seeking controlled pilots.
  • Anti-ICP: needs full institutional operation. Route to Digital Cash.
  • Motion: product-led. Sandbox and docs before the first call.
  • Cycle: weeks to a quarter (Own Programme). One to two quarters (Partner-Led). Two quarters+ (White-Label).
Real example: no named Factory customers in canonical source. The deployment experience is the proof point until the first reference closes.

Digital Cash — already-licensed institutions ready to operate

  • Licence: must already cover digital cash or related activity.
  • Priority markets: tier-2/3 banks following JPM/Citi/BBVA/Santander. EMI/PI fintech clusters.
  • Anti-ICP: unlicensed buyer. No exec sponsor identified.
  • Motion: enterprise-led. The proof point is a multi-stakeholder workshop, not a demo.
  • Cycle: 6–18 months. Phase-1 possible in 4–6 months on narrow scope.
Real example: no named Digital Cash customers in canonical source. Peer references for context only: JPM Coin, Citi Token Services, BBVA, Santander.

An account that does not match an ICP can still be worth a conversation. It counts as relationship work, not pipeline.

What "qualified" means.

Six checks. Same for every product. Without them, the lead is interest, not pipeline.

  1. Use case — one sentence the customer can repeat. "Exploring" is not a use case.
  2. Decision owner — named, by title. The right one for the product. Digital Cash does not close without an executive sponsor.
  3. Budget framing — how the money gets found. Card P&L impact (PbB). Motion-fit pricing (Factory). Multi-year capex/opex (Digital Cash).
  4. Trigger event — why this quarter, not next year. VAMP threshold. Funding round. Compliance deadline. Peer move.
  5. Licence position — customer holds the licence. Aryze is infrastructure, never issuer or operator. Factory and Digital Cash depend on this. PbB merchants operate under their own licence.
  6. Technical capacity — an owner who can take it forward. Payment owner + dev (PbB). Technical team for sandbox (Factory). Treasury + legal + compliance + tech engaged together (Digital Cash).

Three lead sources feed the pipeline.

The channel decides warmth. The qualification standard decides whether it becomes pipeline.

Source 1

Conferences — warm and cold

Warm leads are people and companies we had a relevant conversation with at the event, where interest was shown. They are followed up fast — typically by mail shortly after the meeting, with a sandbox invitation and a customized deck built from the actual conversation (needs, interests, what was discussed — never generic). Cold leads are attendees from the same conference we did not get to. The attendee list identifies them; the shared attendance — "we were both at the same event" — is the warm way into an otherwise cold contact.

Source 2

Agents — one vertical each

Agents are specialised per product and per industry — e.g. iGaming/digital, finance, banks. Each works the vertical where their industry and product knowledge is strongest; for Pay by Bank they reach into the high-risk merchant ICP through their own strongest vertical. Agents typically join us at conferences. (Per-agent vertical, strengths and target list: the "Agent profiles" document — an open slot, not yet written.)

Source 3

Inbound / marketing

Customers who find us through marketing and show interest themselves. Followed up like any lead — qualification still applies. (Detail open: which channels drive inbound, who owns follow-up, how an inbound enquiry is qualified.)

An agent introduction is useful only when it answers four questions: why this account, why this product, who owns it, and by when.

How leads are worked: juniors qualify, seniors close.

After a conference the attendee list is reviewed and cold leads go to the sales juniors, who assess potential, set up the follow-up call where relevant, and hand qualified leads to the sales lead / director. Roles in full: Sales team — who does what.

Juniors join the calls

Follow-up calls are not run alone — including on warm leads. A junior typically joins the responsible seller: they learn the sales dialogue, can carry the follow-up afterwards, and see how highly interested leads are handled.

The boundary: when the merchant agreement is sent, the deal is commercially won and the customer hands over to onboarding — where KYB and technical integration start in parallel. Sales ends at the agreement; onboarding owns everything after it.

Flexible where needed: follow-ups are expected when buyer, value, risk or stakeholder clarity is needed. Strict on progression: no opportunity advances without product path, owner and next milestone.

Top objections.

The three heard most often, per product, with the honest answer to each.

Top objections per product — the honest answer to each

Pay by Bank — three to expect

  • "We already have an open-banking provider." The answer is the specific job their provider does not fill: theming, pricing, market depth.
  • "Our chargeback rate is fine." The number is what matters, against VAMP April 2026 (1.5% threshold, acquirers hold at 0.5–0.7%).
  • "What about payouts?" Not productised today. Aryze covers deposits, top-ups and customer funding.

Stablecoin Factory — three to expect

  • "Why Aryze and not Fireblocks, Circle or Brale?" The answer is self-serve speed plus the path upward to Digital Cash.
  • "Can Aryze issue the stablecoin?" No. The customer is issuer.
  • "We need settlement, treasury and ramps day one." That need belongs with Digital Cash.

Digital Cash — three to expect

  • "Why not build internally?" The reality is 12–24 months and 5+ FTEs. Aryze is a partnership, not a replacement.
  • "How do we know this is regulator-friendly?" The institution remains issuer and operator, and an MLRO and GC workshop is available.
  • "How fast can we be live?" Honestly, 6–18 months. Phase-1 in 4–6 months on narrow scope.
